Climate shocks, household food security and welfare in Afghanistan

Hayatullah Ahmadzai and Oliver Morrissey. 2025. Food Policy, 134, 102910. DOI: 10.1016/j.foodpol.2025.102910

Afghan households face growing exposure to floods and other climate shocks layered on top of conflict and price volatility. Drawing on two household surveys, this paper uses endogenous switching regressions and matching methods to isolate the effect of flooding on food security, dietary diversity, farm revenue, and income — and shows that conflict makes the damage from floods worse. Affected households see food consumption spending fall by roughly a third, with similar drops in income and farm revenue, underscoring the need for disaster risk reduction strategies tailored to fragile settings.

Crop Diversification and Farm Household Welfare during Conflict: Afghanistan 2011–2017

Hayatullah Ahmadzai and Oliver Morrissey. 2025. The Journal of Development Studies, 61(3), 381–399. DOI: 10.1080/00220388.2024.2404576

Examines whether diversifying crop production helps Afghan farm households withstand conflict-related shocks, using panel data spanning a period of intensifying violence. The findings speak to how household-level production choices interact with insecurity to shape welfare outcomes.

Hayatullah Ahmadzai. 2024. Journal of Social and Economic Development. DOI: 10.1007/s40847-024-00364-2

Empirical Analysis of Impacts of Sand and Dust Storms on Agricultural Productivity in Mongolia

Hayatullah Ahmadzai, A. Malhotra, and S. Tutundjian. 2023. PLOS ONE, 18(2): e0269271. DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0269271

Quantifies how sand and dust storm exposure affects agricultural productivity in Mongolia, contributing empirical evidence to a literature that has mostly focused on the physical and atmospheric dimensions of these events rather than their economic costs to farmers.

Companion analysis to the Mongolia paper, examining the agricultural productivity costs of sand and dust storms in Iraq’s farming regions.

Hayatullah Ahmadzai. 2021. Progress in Development Studies, 21(3), 1–25. DOI: 10.1177/14649934211031745
